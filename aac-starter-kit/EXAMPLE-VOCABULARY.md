> **These are examples, not prescriptions.** Choose words based on YOUR child's daily life, not this list. A speech-language pathologist can help you prioritize. The best vocabulary is the one your child actually needs to say, every day.

## Quick phrases (19 suggested)

One-tap sentences for common daily needs. Organized by Light & McNaughton's 4 communicative purposes:

### Needs and wants
- mau nonton TV (want to watch TV)
- mau keluar (want to go out)
- mau tidur (want to sleep)
- mau di rumah (want to stay home)
- tidak mau (don't want)
- aku lapar (I'm hungry)
- mau [person] (want [mom/dad])

### Social closeness
- halo (hello)
- dadah (bye-bye)
- aku sayang kamu (I love you)
- terima kasih (thank you)
- maaf (sorry)

### Social etiquette
- selamat pagi (good morning)
- selamat malam (good night)
- permisi (excuse me)

### Information + repair
- aku tidak tahu (I don't know)
- apa itu (what's that)
- tunggu sebentar (wait a moment)
- aku capek (I'm tired)

## Language notes for Bahasa Indonesia

### Register
- Use informal register for a child's AAC: `aku` not `saya`, `kamu` not `Anda`, `nggak` alongside `tidak`.
- Exception: `permisi`, `terima kasih`, `maaf` — these are polite forms and appropriate in both registers.

### TTS
- Web Speech API language code: `id-ID`
- Most Android devices have an Indonesian TTS voice pre-installed (Google TTS).
- If the voice sounds robotic, check Settings → Accessibility → Text-to-speech → install a higher-quality voice pack.

### Morphology (advanced, future feature)
Indonesian morphology is prefix/suffix based: `me-`, `ber-`, `di-`, `-kan`, `-lah`, `-nya`. A future version could offer tap-and-hold for word forms (e.g., hold `makan` → `dimakan`, `makanan`, `memakan`). This is not implemented in the base build guide.
